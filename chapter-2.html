Chapter 2: Written Skills

Business Communication

1. Introduction to Written Communication

Written communication is a form of verbal communication involving the transfer of messages through the written or printed word. It is used both formally (reports, business proposals, contracts) and informally (text messages, handwritten notes, quick emails).

Written communication serves as a permanent record and is essential in business for documentation, legal purposes, and clear transmission of complex information. Unlike oral communication, it allows the sender time to carefully craft their message and the receiver time to process and review the content.

Advantages vs. Disadvantages of Written Communication

Advantages Disadvantages
Precise and helpful for maintaining records. Time-consuming to plan and execute.
Holds legal validity. Immediate feedback is unavailable.
Can be read multiple times for clarity. High cost due to stationery and paperwork.
Allows scope for editing and proofreading. Non-verbal cues (tone/gestures) are absent.

2. Planning the Message: The 7Cs Checklist

To be effective, written messages must observe seven prerequisites, often referred to as the 7Cs of Communication. These principles ensure that your message achieves its intended purpose and maintains professionalism.

Mnemonic to remember the 7Cs: "C-4, Co-2, T" (Complete, Clear, Correct, Concise, Concrete, Coherent, and courTeous).

Principle Description Example Application
Complete Must not lack any relevant information to avoid misinterpretation. Include all details like dates, amounts, contact information, and action items in meeting minutes.
Clear Use unambiguous language and have a clear purpose. Instead of "ASAP," specify "by Friday, 3 PM." Avoid jargon unless the audience understands it.
Correct Statements must be accurate and grammatically sound. Verify facts, figures, names, and titles. Use proper grammar, spelling, and punctuation.
Concise Avoid exaggerating details; every statement should be meaningful. Replace "at this point in time" with "now." Eliminate filler phrases and redundancies.
Concrete Use facts and figures to make the message impactful. Say "Sales increased by 23%" instead of "Sales improved significantly."
Coherent The message must flow logically through an introduction, body, and conclusion. Use transition words, maintain topic consistency, and organize ideas in a logical sequence.
Courteous The tone must be polite and considerate of the audience's sensibilities. Use "please" and "thank you." Phrase negatives positively: "We will process your request by Monday" instead of "We cannot help you until Monday."

3. Executing Different Types of Messages

A successful writer adopts a "reader-centred" or "you" approach, focusing on the audience's needs, interests, and benefits rather than their own perspective. This approach increases engagement and improves the likelihood of achieving your communication goals.

Request Messages

Request messages are typically claim letters or formal inquiries that open with a direct request followed by supporting arguments and justification. The key is to be clear about what you need while providing context.

Structure: State the request upfront, explain why you're making it, provide necessary details, and close with a polite call to action.

Example: "I am writing to request a copy of my employment contract dated March 15, 2024. I need this document for my loan application process. Could you please email it to me by the end of this week?"

Positive Messages

Positive messages deliver good news where the receiver will be pleased with the content. These include congratulations, approvals, acceptances, and commendations.

Structure: Lead with the good news immediately (direct approach), provide details in the middle, and end on a positive note.

Examples: Promotion announcements, scholarship awards, contract approvals, performance bonuses, acceptance letters.

Negative Messages

Negative messages communicate unfavourable news such as refusals, rejections, or denials. The key principle is to soften the blow while maintaining honesty and professionalism.

Buffer Approach: Begin with a neutral or positive statement, explain the reasoning clearly, deliver the negative news, and offer alternatives or express goodwill.

Example: An electronics company responds to a customer whose ordered item is out of stock: "Thank you for your order. We appreciate your interest in our XYZ model. Unfortunately, this item is currently unavailable due to high demand. As a gesture of goodwill, we're offering you a 20% discount coupon valid on any product in our store, plus priority notification when the item is restocked."

This approach maintains the customer relationship rather than simply stating "Item out of stock."

Persuasive Messages

Persuasive messages are designed to move a disinterested or skeptical receiver toward a desired action. These require strong evidence, logical argumentation, and an understanding of audience motivation.

AIDA Model for Persuasion:

  • Attention: Capture interest with a compelling opening
  • Interest: Build engagement by highlighting benefits
  • Desire: Create want through evidence and emotional appeal
  • Action: Provide a clear, easy next step

Applications: Sales letters, fundraising appeals, policy change proposals, marketing campaigns, job applications.

4. Report Writing

Derived from the Latin reportare ("to bring back"), a report is a structured document that presents details of an event, research, or analysis in an organised manner for a specific receiver or audience. Reports are fundamental to business decision-making and accountability.

Classification of Reports

By Purpose

Informational Reports: Present objective facts without personal opinions or recommendations. They describe "what happened" without interpretation. Examples include annual reports, sales reports, meeting minutes, progress reports, and inventory summaries.

Analytical Reports: Include not only facts but also observations, interpretations, conclusions, and recommendations to solve problems. These reports answer "what does it mean?" and "what should we do?" Examples include feasibility studies, market research analysis, problem-solving reports, and strategic recommendations.

By Direction

Vertical Reports: Travel up or down the corporate hierarchy. Upward reports (subordinate to superior) include progress updates and performance reports. Downward reports (superior to subordinate) include policy directives and performance reviews.

Lateral Reports: Move horizontally across departments at the same hierarchical level. These facilitate coordination between teams, such as inter-departmental project updates or resource-sharing communications.

By Audience

Internal Reports: Stay within the organisation and address internal stakeholders. Examples include HR reports, operational reports, and budget reports.

External Reports: Directed to stakeholders outside the organisation such as shareholders, government regulators, clients, or the public. Examples include annual reports to shareholders, regulatory compliance reports, and client project reports.

Report Formats

Format Usage Key Elements
Letter Format Used for external or long reports sent to recipients outside the organization. Heading, Date, Receiver's Address, Subject Line, Salutation, Body (Introduction, Findings, Conclusion), Complimentary Close, Signature
Memo Format Short for "memorandum"; used for internal records and quick communications within the organization. Heading, Title (e.g., "Interoffice Memorandum"), Date, To, From, Subject, Body (direct and concise), Reference Initials (optional)

Essential Components of Formal Reports

  • Title Page: Report title, author, date, recipient
  • Executive Summary: Brief overview of findings and recommendations (written last but appears first)
  • Table of Contents: Navigation aid for longer reports
  • Introduction: Background, purpose, scope, methodology
  • Body: Detailed findings organized by sections with headings
  • Conclusions: Interpretation of findings
  • Recommendations: Actionable suggestions based on analysis
  • Appendices: Supporting data, charts, raw data, detailed calculations

5. Improving Personal Writing Skills

Even with brilliant ideas, grammatical errors and poor writing mechanics can leave a negative impression and damage professional credibility and business relationships. Attention to writing fundamentals is essential.

Sentence Structure Fundamentals

A complete sentence consists of two essential components:

  • Subject: The noun or pronoun that performs the action (the "doer")
  • Predicate: The verb and any words that complete the thought (the action or description)

Example: "The manager [subject] approved the budget proposal [predicate]."

Common Writing Pitfalls

Run-on Sentences

Run-on sentences occur when independent clauses are joined without proper punctuation or conjunctions. They create confusion and reduce readability.

Incorrect: "The meeting was scheduled for 2 PM everyone attended on time."

Correct Options:

  • Use a period: "The meeting was scheduled for 2 PM. Everyone attended on time."
  • Use a semicolon: "The meeting was scheduled for 2 PM; everyone attended on time."
  • Use a comma with conjunction: "The meeting was scheduled for 2 PM, and everyone attended on time."

Sentence Fragments

Sentence fragments are incomplete sentences that lack either a subject, a verb, or fail to express a complete thought. While acceptable in casual speech, they should be avoided in formal writing.

Incorrect: "Because the deadline was approaching." (Fragment - incomplete thought)

Correct: "We worked overtime because the deadline was approaching." (Complete sentence)

Parallel Structure

Parallel structure (parallelism) means using the same grammatical pattern when listing items or ideas. This creates rhythm, improves clarity, and makes writing more professional.

Incorrect: "Sarah enjoys dancing, singing, and to hike." (Mixed forms)

Correct: "Sarah enjoys dancing, singing, and hiking." (All gerunds)

Another example:

  • Incorrect: "The company values innovation, being creative, and to take risks."
  • Correct: "The company values innovation, creativity, and risk-taking." (All nouns)

Paragraph Construction

A well-constructed paragraph is the building block of effective writing. Each paragraph should focus on one central topic or idea, known as the controlling idea.

Effective Paragraph Structure:

  • Topic Sentence: States the main idea (usually first sentence)
  • Supporting Sentences: Develop the idea with facts, examples, or explanations
  • Concluding/Transition Sentence: Wraps up the idea or leads to the next paragraph

Transition Words and Phrases

Transitions connect ideas within and between paragraphs, creating logical flow and coherence.

Purpose Transition Words
Addition Furthermore, moreover, in addition to, additionally, also, besides
Contrast However, nevertheless, on the other hand, conversely, in contrast, despite
Cause/Effect Therefore, consequently, as a result, thus, hence, accordingly
Sequence First, second, next, then, finally, subsequently, meanwhile
Example For instance, for example, such as, specifically, to illustrate
Conclusion In conclusion, to summarize, in summary, ultimately, in brief

6. The Writing Process

For substantial compositions like essays, research papers, or comprehensive reports, professional writers follow a systematic three-stage process. This approach ensures quality, coherence, and effectiveness.

Stage 1: Prewriting (Planning)

This critical stage lays the foundation for successful writing. Rushing through prewriting often results in disorganized, unfocused documents.

Key Activities:

  • Brainstorming: Generate ideas without judgment; use mind maps, lists, or free writing
  • Research: Gather necessary information, data, and sources
  • Audience Analysis: Identify reader needs, knowledge level, and expectations
  • Purpose Definition: Clarify what you want to achieve
  • Thesis Statement: Develop a clear central argument or main point that guides the entire document
  • Outlining: Organize ideas into a logical structure with main points and supporting details

Example Thesis Statement: "Implementing flexible work arrangements will increase employee productivity, reduce operational costs, and improve employee satisfaction."

Stage 2: Writing (Drafting)

During this stage, transform your outline into complete sentences and paragraphs. Focus on getting ideas down; perfection comes later during editing.

Introduction

The introduction serves multiple purposes: capture attention, establish context, and present the thesis. Effective techniques include:

  • Start with a compelling statistic or fact
  • Pose a thought-provoking question
  • Share a relevant anecdote or scenario
  • State the problem or issue clearly
  • Provide necessary background information

Body

The body develops your thesis through multiple paragraphs, each focused on a specific aspect of your argument or analysis.

Best Practices:

  • Use topic sentences to introduce each paragraph's main idea
  • Support claims with facts, figures, examples, and evidence
  • Maintain one idea per paragraph
  • Use transitions to connect paragraphs logically
  • Vary sentence structure for readability
  • Include relevant data visualizations (charts, graphs, tables) where appropriate

Conclusion

The conclusion brings closure and reinforces your main points. Effective conclusions:

  • Summarize key findings or arguments without introducing new information
  • Restate the thesis in fresh language
  • Emphasize implications or significance
  • Provide recommendations or call to action
  • End with a memorable closing statement

Signal Phrases: "Therefore," "In conclusion," "To summarize," "Ultimately," "In light of this evidence"

Stage 3: Editing (Revising and Proofreading)

Editing transforms a rough draft into polished, professional writing. This stage requires multiple passes, each with a different focus.

Content Editing (First Pass)

  • Verify all information is accurate and current
  • Ensure arguments are logical and well-supported
  • Check that thesis is clear and consistently developed
  • Confirm all sections contribute to the purpose
  • Remove irrelevant or redundant content

Structural Editing (Second Pass)

  • Evaluate overall organization and flow
  • Verify paragraphs follow logical sequence
  • Check transition effectiveness
  • Ensure topic sentences align with paragraph content
  • Confirm introduction and conclusion bracket the content effectively

Copy Editing (Third Pass)

  • Correct grammar, punctuation, and spelling errors
  • Improve sentence structure and word choice
  • Eliminate jargon or define technical terms
  • Ensure consistent verb tense and point of view
  • Replace weak verbs and vague language with specific, strong alternatives
  • Check that tone matches audience and purpose

Proofreading (Final Pass)

  • Catch remaining typos and formatting inconsistencies
  • Verify all names, dates, and figures are correct
  • Check formatting (fonts, headings, spacing, margins)
  • Ensure all citations and references are complete and properly formatted
  • Read aloud to catch awkward phrasing

Pro Tip: Take a break between writing and editing. Fresh eyes catch more errors and identify improvement opportunities more effectively.
