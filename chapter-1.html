Chapter 1: Communication in Organisations

Business Communication

1. Introduction to Communication

Communication is derived from the Latin word communicare, meaning 'to share' or 'make common'. It is the process of sharing and understanding meaning. In a business environment, it is the mechanism that ensures an organisation functions smoothly by addressing internal and external factors.

Daily Life Example: Meaning and Context

In the US, the term "bike" typically refers to a bicycle, whereas in India, it almost always refers to a motorbike. This demonstrates how meaning is tied to cultural context.

2. The Communication Process

Communication is a cyclical process involving multiple actors, processes, and potential interference. The cycle completes when feedback returns to the original sender.

Actors

Sender: The individual or entity initiating the communication by formulating a thought or idea to share.

Receiver: The individual or entity who receives and interprets the transmitted message.

Processes

Encoding: The sender converts thoughts into communicable form using language, symbols, gestures, or other sign systems.

Message: The encoded content itself, which can be verbal, written, visual, or non-verbal in nature.

Channel: The medium through which the message travels. Examples include email, face-to-face conversation, phone calls, or video conferencing.

Decoding: The receiver interprets the message based on their own knowledge, experience, and cultural context.

Feedback: The receiver's response, which may be verbal, non-verbal, or written. Feedback transforms the receiver into a sender, creating a cyclical exchange.

Interference

Noise: Any factor that distorts or impedes the message. This includes:

  • Physical noise: Background sounds, poor audio quality, visual distractions
  • Psychological noise: Preconceptions, biases, emotional states
  • Semantic noise: Jargon, ambiguous terms, language barriers
  • Technical noise: Equipment failure, connectivity issues

The Cyclical Flow

Sender encodes message → Message transmitted through channel → Receiver decodes message → Feedback encoded by receiver → Original sender decodes feedback. Noise can occur at any stage of this cycle.

3. The 7Cs of Effective Communication

These seven principles act as a checklist for successful messaging.

Mnemonic to remember the 7Cs: "C-4, Co-2, T" (Clear, Concise, Concrete, Correct, Coherent, Complete, Courteous).

Principle Description
Clear Avoid subjective interpretation; get straight to the point.
Concise Keep it brief; weed out unnecessary "fillers" and repetitions.
Concrete Use specific facts and figures; avoid flowery, embellished language.
Correct Ensure the message is factually accurate and grammatically sound.
Coherent Present ideas in a logical sequence.
Complete Provide all necessary information to prevent follow-up questions.
Courteous Use a polite, empathetic tone to improve audience receptivity.

4. Corporate Communication

Corporate communication involves engaging with both internal and external stakeholders.

Stakeholders

  • Internal: Employees, Management, Board of Directors, Shareholders.
  • External: Customers, Suppliers, Media, Regulators, Competitors.

Key Functions

  • Public Relations (PR): Managing the brand image via press releases and media relations.
  • Crisis Communication: Swift strategic response to events that could damage reputation (e.g., accidents or fraud).
  • Internal Communication: Training, internal publications, and sensitising employees to company decisions.

5. Listening Skills

Listening is an active, conscious mind process, whereas hearing is merely the physical perception of sound vibrations.

Stages of Listening

  1. Receiving: Filtering out background noise to hear the message.
  2. Understanding: Interpreting the message (the most critical stage).
  3. Remembering: Memorising key details for engagement.
  4. Evaluating: Making value judgements based on perception and bias.
  5. Feedback: Codifying a response to complete the process.

Types of Listening

  • Active Listening: Ideal form; seeks to understand meaning exactly as intended.
  • Pseudo-Listening: Portraying attentiveness while actually ignoring the speaker.
  • Selective Listening: Filtering out everything except what interests the listener.
  • Insulated Listening: Actively avoiding certain pieces of information.
  • Stage Hogging: Interrupting to make oneself the centre of attention.

6. Verbal and Non-Verbal Skills

Non-Verbal: Proxemics (Personal Space)

Edward T. Hall identified four distinct space zones used in communication:

  1. Intimate (0-1.5 ft): For touching/embracing.
  2. Personal (1.5-4 ft): For friends and family.
  3. Social (4-12 ft): For acquaintances and business settings.
  4. Public (12-25+ ft): For public speaking and speeches.

Body Language (Kinesics)

  • Oculesics: The study of eye contact.
  • Haptics: Communication through touch.
  • Paralanguage: Acoustic features like pitch, tone, and modulation.

7. Presentation Skills (The 5 Ws)

To put together an effective presentation, you must answer:

  • Who? Audience age, education, and relationship to speaker.
  • Why? Purpose (informational, change-based, or fundraising).
  • Where? Venue size, logistics, and ambient noise.
  • When? Time of day (e.g., slot before lunch vs. last slot of the day).
  • What? Level of detail and visual content required.

8. Legal Issues in Communication

Everything an employee says or writes can have legal implications.

  • Marketing: Must follow ASCI (Advertising Standards Council of India) guidelines to ensure truthfulness and decency.
  • Disclaimers: Protect against liability for misuse of products (e.g., "Mutual fund investments are subject to market risks").
  • Intellectual Property Rights (IPR): Avoiding unauthorised use of patents, trademarks, or copyrighted writing.
  • Financial Reporting: Strictly regulated; misrepresentation leads to criminal penalties.
